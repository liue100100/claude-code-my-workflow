## Summary Assessment

**Recommendation: MAJOR REVISION.**

The paper documents a genuinely interesting and policy-relevant phenomenon: under South Australia's direction-compensation scheme — a gross payment at a trailing-365-day 90th-percentile reference price — the three Torrens Island B units held a standing withdrawn posture, were directed into presence at their operating floor, and were paid $141.9M for directed output with $7.2M of spot value. The headline empirical claim is a dose response: the essential-versus-matched withholding gap widens by ~5.1 pp of registered capacity per $100/MWh of the compensation price (RQ2), a margin no ordinary market-power model prices. The institutional forensics are excellent, the data validation against AEMO's published series is convincing, the self-correction of a contaminated day-grain result is exemplary research practice, and the design section is unusually candid about power and cluster structure.

The problem is that the paper's causal centerpiece rests on cross-month covariation between a slow-moving administered price and a slow-moving conduct gap, with 21 effective clusters, half the identifying mass in three months, and a treatment variable that is a mechanical transform of trailing spot prices — which correlate with fuel stress, portfolio scarcity value, and the units' own conduct (a feedback loop the paper never confronts). The paper acknowledges the confounding class in one paragraph and offers "mitigation, not proof," but the mitigation offered is thin relative to what the setting permits: no placebo on a non-eligible unit's gap, no horse race against the contemporaneous fuel margin, no exploitation of the formula's sharpest feature (the mechanical roll-off of the 2022 crisis out of the trailing window in mid-2023), and no month-level visualization of the gap against d_t. There is also an unresolved internal tension between the mechanism section (the posture is standing, day-block, indifferent to the daily envelope) and the interval-grain design (which measures within-month essential-versus-matched gaps): if conduct does not flex within the month, the interval-grain estimand is *alignment* of a standing absence with rivals' shortfall, not a conduct response, and the paper should say precisely what θ then measures. Inference relies on an unrestricted wild-cluster bootstrap in exactly the few-and-unbalanced-cluster regime where the null-imposed variant is known to matter. Finally, the welfare framing (the 20:1 headline; "the scheme bought its own necessity") values directed output at spot rather than at the security service actually procured, and never engages the most obvious counterfactual — that without the scheme these loss-making units mothball or retire and the security problem gets worse before the synchronous condensers arrive.

None of these problems looks fatal. The dose response may well survive; the requested diagnostics are feasible with data in hand; and the descriptive and mechanism contributions stand on their own. But as written, the identification claim outruns the evidence, and the literature positioning misses the closest neighbors (inc-dec gaming, withholding-via-outages, strategic behavior in capacity/reserve mechanisms, the divisible-good auction tradition). Major revision.

## Strengths

1. **A margin the literature genuinely does not price.** The observation that essentiality bundles compensation-eligibility with market power, and that only the payment channel predicts a dose response to the administered rate, is a sharp and correct identification idea. The RQ1/RQ2 architecture — level effect confounded, interaction identifying — is well conceived.

2. **Institutional forensics of high quality.** The gross-payment characterization (compensation ≈ 0.95 × directed MWh × d_t, R² = 0.99), the backward-looking price anatomy (Fig. 1), the directed-output-equals-floor result (Fig. 4, 55.9% at or below zero excess), and the roster-never-short-of-the-standard result (73/73) together constitute the best empirical description of an out-of-market direction scheme I have seen.

3. **External validation.** Reproducing AEMO's Quarterly Energy Dynamics share-of-time-directed series and matching the Q2-2023 published cost to machine precision materially raises confidence in the event record.

4. **Exemplary self-correction.** The contamination audit — the withdrawn −12.7 pp day-grain "state dependence" that was the direction itself sitting in the measured bids — is reported in full, with the clean-day rule then applied throughout. The withdrawn Pelican Point coefficient is retained, footnoted, in T1. This is how empirical work should be done.

5. **Honest power accounting.** The 21 effective clusters, the 50.8%-in-three-months concentration, and the d_t variation (SD $76.6) are reported *with* the result, not buried; the day-grain null is framed as an uninformative bound rather than suppressed.

6. **The wrong-sign-of-conditions result** (essential intervals carry *more* rival supply near the clearing price, −5.04 vs −3.31) is a clean, memorable way to discipline the energy-market-power alternative — though see MC5 on whether it fully dispatches the portfolio version of that alternative.

## Major Concerns

### MC1. The RQ2 interaction is identified off cross-month variation that the design cannot separate from everything else that moved across those months

θ is identified from covariation between the monthly d_t and the monthly essential-versus-matched gap, with month FE absorbing d_t levels. The paper concedes ("Mitigation, not proof") that anything widening the gap and co-moving with d_t across months confounds — and names 2022H2 fuel stress. But the concession understates the problem in three ways:

- **d_t is a deterministic transform of trailing spot prices.** It is therefore mechanically correlated with every 12-month moving summary of market conditions: gas prices, scarcity, the profitability of AGL's SA portfolio, and — see MC2 — the focal units' own conduct. "Excluding June 2022 leaves the estimate unchanged" rules out one month, not a year-long moving average of the crisis.
- **The effective variation is coarser than 21 clusters.** A trailing-365-day percentile has essentially four regimes in-sample (pre-crisis ramp, the $348–350 plateau to mid-2023, the roll-off, the 2024 drift up; Fig. 1). The identifying variation is closer to a handful of regime segments than 21 independent draws. Fig. 5 shows stability across June-2022 treatments — the least demanding robustness axis, since the plateau, not June, carries the variation.
- **Secular drift in the outcome coincides with the plateau.** Section 4 itself reports that 2023 — the rent peak — is "the deepest year of absence" including three months at literally zero cheap MW. One downward trend in Torrens availability over 2022–2023 (aging plant, maintenance economics, announced retirement path) plus the crisis plateau in d_t generates a negative θ with no payment-seeking at all.

**What would move me substantially, all feasible with data in hand:**
(a) a month-by-month plot of the essential-versus-matched gap against d_t (36 points), so the reader sees which segments carry θ;
(b) a **placebo on a compensation-ineligible margin** — Pelican Point's (or the pooled non-Torrens) essential-versus-matched gap interacted with d_t; the payment story predicts nothing there, the fuel-stress story predicts the same sign;
(c) a **horse race**: essential × contemporaneous fuel margin (or gas price) alongside essential × d_t — the two series diverge sharply during the 2023 plateau (Fig. 1's whole point), which is precisely the variation that can separate them;
(d) exploit the formula's sharpest feature: the mechanical **roll-off of the crisis out of the trailing window in mid-2023**, when d_t fell ~$120 while fuel costs were flat — an event-study of the gap around the roll-off is the cleanest quasi-experiment this setting offers and the paper does not use it.

### MC2. The compensation price is not exogenous to the conduct it allegedly causes

d_t is the 90th percentile of trailing SA spot prices. The focal units are, per the paper's own essentiality analysis, at times indispensable; their standing absence removes up to 600 MW from the SA supply stack and mechanically shifts the spot distribution's upper tail — the very tail the 90th percentile samples. The scheme therefore contains a feedback loop the paper never states: withholding → higher spot percentile → higher d_t → (per θ) more withholding. Over cross-month horizons this is not a curiosity; it is a simultaneity problem in the paper's central regression. At minimum the paper must (i) acknowledge the loop, (ii) quantify the plausible pass-through of the units' absence to the trailing percentile (a bounding exercise on the spot distribution with and without withheld capacity would do), and (iii) discuss whether θ should be read as a reduced-form equilibrium object rather than a conduct elasticity. Ideally, instrument the d_t variation with the component driven by the *east-coast gas price* entering the trailing window — variation plausibly external to SA conduct.

### MC3. "Attenuation toward zero" from realised-state classification is asserted, not established — and the essentiality flag may itself be an equilibrium object

Two distinct problems:

- The lower-bound claim (§5.2, repeated in §6.2: "the true payment-sensitivity is if anything larger") invokes classical-measurement-error logic. But the misclassification here is nonclassical: essentiality is realised rivals' availability, and rivals' availability responds to the same weather, fuel, and outage shocks as the focal unit's conduct. For an *interaction* coefficient in a matched design, correlated misclassification can bias in either direction. Either prove the attenuation claim under stated assumptions, or delete the lower-bound language and present θ as-is. This matters because the abstract and results section both lean on it.
- The rivals-only construction is defended by an interval-level leakage audit (R² ≤ 0.0028 on the unit's own behaviour). But the mechanism section's own finding is that conduct operates at the *day-block and roster* level. A standing three-unit absence changes rivals' commitment incentives (they face higher prices and more residual demand) over weeks and months — equilibrium feedback that an interval-level R² cannot detect. If rivals' availability partially accommodates the focal station's posture, pex is partly caused by that posture, and essential intervals are selected on the station's own regime. Discuss, and if possible test at the monthly grain (does rivals' aggregate availability respond to lagged Torrens posture?).

### MC4. The estimand tension between the mechanism section and the interval-grain design is real and unresolved

Section 7 establishes — persuasively — that the posture is standing: chosen in multi-day blocks, indifferent to the daily security envelope (exits event-timed, not need-timed) and to the daily prize (floor price unmoved). Section 6's RQ2 design measures a within-month, interval-grain gap between essential and matched non-essential intervals. If conduct does not vary within the month at the daily frequency, then the interval-grain gap is generated by *composition*: essential intervals (rivals short) landing disproportionately on days when the station is already absent. θ < 0 then says that in high-d_t months the station's standing absence is more tightly aligned with rivals' shortfall. That is still consistent with payment-seeking (choosing *which* blocks to be absent in, or how deep the standing posture runs in months when essentiality pays more) — but it is a different estimand from "withholding responds to the payment," and §7.4's "reconciling the grains" discussion (powered estimate vs. uninformative bound) addresses relative *precision*, not the estimand. The revision should: (i) state explicitly what θ measures under the standing-posture model; (ii) decompose the gap into a day-composition component and a within-day component; (iii) report the honest month-grain regression (36 monthly gaps on d_t) as the design-consistent aggregate, wide CIs and all. Related: the serial dependence of 5-minute intervals within multi-day withdrawal spells means the 140,259-row sample contains far fewer independent observations than it appears — month clustering absorbs this formally, but the reader should be told the effective count of essential *spells/days*, not just unit-intervals (12,513 intervals ≈ how many distinct essential days? Appendix C suggests ~186 N-0 essential days in total).

### MC5. The market-power alternative is dismissed on the energy margin but never confronted on the portfolio margin

The wrong-sign-of-conditions argument is good against a single-unit energy-market-power story: essential intervals are more competitive, so withholding-when-essential cannot be classic scarcity exploitation *in those intervals*. But AGL is a vertically integrated gentailer with a multi-plant SA position (Torrens B's sister units, Barker Inlet, a retail book). A standing withdrawal of up to 600 MW raises SA spot prices in *other* hours and raises the value of AGL's remaining generation — the textbook portfolio-withholding mechanism (Wolak's unilateral-market-power framework, which the paper cites, is exactly about this). Crucially for this paper, the portfolio return to withholding co-moves with the price level, and d_t is a trailing transform of the price level — so portfolio withholding can generate a spurious essential × d_t interaction through the MC1 channel. The paper needs: (i) an explicit statement of AGL's SA portfolio and retail position over the window; (ii) an argument or test separating the payment channel from the portfolio channel (the placebo in MC1(b) helps: portfolio withholding does not care about pex; payment-seeking does); (iii) engagement with the rebidding/portfolio literature in the NEM context. Note also that the contemporaneous spot price appears as a *control* in equations (1)–(2) while being an outcome of the conduct under study — a bad-control problem worth a footnote and a no-spot-control robustness column.

### MC6. Inference: the unrestricted wild bootstrap is the wrong variant for exactly this cluster structure, and the choice is excused rather than fixed

The paper's own accounting — 21 effective clusters, 50.8% of the essential mass in three months — is the canonical few-and-unbalanced-clusters regime in which MacKinnon–Webb show unrestricted (non-null-imposed) WCB over-rejects, sometimes badly. The footnote in §5.5 ("the null-imposed implementation could not be installed in the analysis environment. The difference is second-order at 36 clusters") is not acceptable in a paper whose headline p-values are 0.004–0.007: the relevant count is 21 unbalanced, not 36, and the claim of second-orderness is asserted, not shown. `fwildclusterboot` / Stata `boottest` are standard; run the null-imposed WCB-R, and add randomization inference (permute the d_t path across months, or across essential-month assignments) as a design-based check. If the result survives — and it may — the paper is much stronger; if it does not, the reader needs to know now.

### MC7. The welfare framing values the wrong commodity, and the counterfactual is missing

The 20:1 headline ($141.9M paid vs. $7.2M spot value) is rhetorically powerful and analytically loose. The scheme did not buy energy; it bought synchronous presence — inertia and system strength — whose value is the avoided cost of the alternative (load shedding at VOLL, or earlier/larger syncon procurement), not the spot value of the incidental MWh. The paper knows this (§9.2 "procure presence explicitly," the syncon comparison) but the headline framing, repeated in the abstract, introduction, and discussion, invites the objection. Worse, "the scheme bought its own necessity" presumes a counterfactual in which, absent the payments, the units would have been *present*. The paper's own §7.1 shows the market alternative was loss-making in ~96% of directed intervals — so absent compensation, the plausible counterfactual is deeper absence: mothballing or accelerated retirement of a 1960s–70s steam station at the end of its life, and a *worse* security position before the syncons and PEC arrived. On that counterfactual the scheme is an expensive but perhaps unavoidable bridge, and the conduct finding — real as it is — is about the *rent split*, not about induced scarcity. Two paragraphs of §9 acknowledge fragments of this; the revision needs it stated squarely: what does the paper claim the units would have done under (a) wedge-based compensation, (b) no compensation, and what would security provision have cost? A back-of-envelope against the syncons' annualized cost, and against directed-energy value at VOLL rather than spot, would discipline the headline. Relatedly, clarify how the $141.9M is attributed to the three units when unit-level dollars exist only from October 2023 (§3.1 vs §2.2 fn.) — the reader cannot currently reconstruct the headline number's provenance.

### MC8. Literature positioning misses the closest neighbors

The bibliography has 15 entries; for a paper claiming to open a new conduct margin, the mapping of adjacent territory is too thin, and several literatures a referee will name are absent:

- **Divisible-good auction theory of electricity markets:** von der Fehr & Harbord (1993, EJ); Fabra, von der Fehr & Harbord (2006, RAND). The withholding-as-quantity-strategy idea has a theoretical home; the paper should locate its "availability margin" relative to it.
- **Complex bids / unit-commitment costs:** Reguant (2014, ReStud). Directly relevant twice over: start-up and no-load costs are the innocent explanation for bimodal all-or-nothing availability of old steam units, and Reguant's framework shows how commitment costs shape offer behaviour. The paper's SRMC-based loss accounting implicitly assumes away commitment costs; the innocent-explanation section should confront them explicitly.
- **Withholding measured off the energy margin:** capacity withholding via declared outages/failures — Fogelberg & Lazarczyk (2017); Bergler, Heim & Hüschelrath (2017) on strategic withholding around German reserve/redispatch procurement. These are the nearest empirical cousins of "absence as conduct" and are uncited.
- **Inc-dec / out-of-market gaming:** Hirth & Schlecht (2019/2020) on market-based redispatch inducing strategic positioning to be paid out-of-market. This is the same economic mechanism as the paper's — position yourself so the operator must buy you out — and the contribution should be differentiated from it (the SA twist: the payment price is administered and backward-looking, enabling the dose-response test).
- **Strategic conduct in capacity/availability mechanisms:** Teirilä & Ritz (2019) on the Irish capacity market; Schwenen; the RMR repricing literature beyond the two 1999 Bushnell–Wolak reports.
- **NEM-specific:** the Australian rebidding/market-power literature and prior work on SA directions and system strength (AER state-of-the-market analyses are cited; academic NEM work beyond one Simshauser piece is not). A paper about the NEM that cites almost no NEM scholarship will be flagged by any antipodean referee.
- **Bushnell, Mansur & Saravia (2008, AER)** and **Ito & Reguant (2016, AER)** for the vertical-position and sequential-market context of MC5.

## Referee Objections (with why-it-matters and how-to-address)

### RO1. "Your treatment is a moving average of the confounder."
d_t is a deterministic trailing transform of spot prices; spot prices embed fuel stress, scarcity, and the units' own conduct. An essential × d_t interaction identified across 21 unbalanced month-clusters cannot, by design, be distinguished from essential × (any 12-month moving summary of market stress). **Why it matters:** this is the paper's only causal result; everything else is description and mechanism. If θ is fuel stress in disguise, the paper is a (very good) descriptive study of a badly designed scheme, not evidence that conduct responds to the payment. **How to address:** (i) placebo interaction on compensation-ineligible units' essential-gap (payment story predicts zero; stress story predicts same sign); (ii) horse race against essential × contemporaneous fuel margin, exploiting the 2023 divergence between d_t and cost; (iii) event-study around the mid-2023 mechanical roll-off of the crisis from the trailing window — the formula's own discontinuity, unused; (iv) plot the 36 monthly gaps against d_t so the reader sees the identifying segments.

### RO2. "Your mechanism section contradicts your identification grain."
Section 7 shows the posture is standing at the day-block level and indifferent to daily conditions; the RQ2 design identifies θ from interval-level essential-versus-matched gaps within months. Under the paper's own mechanism, that gap is day-composition alignment, not intra-month conduct response. **Why it matters:** the paper's headline sentence — "withholding responds to the administered payment" — is an elasticity claim; if the true object is "the standing posture's depth/alignment across months tracks d_t," the estimand, the magnitude's interpretation (5.1 pp per $100/MWh of *what margin*), and the policy arithmetic all change. **How to address:** state the estimand under the standing-posture model; decompose θ into day-composition and within-day components; report the month-grain aggregate regression as the design-consistent version; reconcile explicitly with the day-grain dose-response null of §7.4 (currently waved through as a power issue).

### RO3. "You valued security at the spot price of energy, then declared the scheme a failure."
The 20:1 headline compares a payment for synchronous presence with the energy value of the incidental MWh, and "the scheme bought its own necessity" assumes the no-scheme counterfactual is presence rather than mothballing/retirement of an end-of-life station whose market operation was loss-making in ~96% of directed intervals. **Why it matters:** the policy conclusions — and the paper's title — rest on this framing; a discussant will dismantle it in one slide if the comparison set (syncon cost, VOLL-valued security, retirement counterfactual) is absent. **How to address:** re-benchmark the transfer against (a) annualized syncon cost per unit of presence, (b) directed-energy value at VOLL under plausible shortfall probabilities; state the retirement counterfactual and what each proposed repair (wedge payment, forward-looking price, availability tender) would have paid the units; recast the contribution as rent-split-and-incentive-design rather than pure waste. Clarify the pre-Oct-2023 attribution behind the $141.9M.

### RO4. "Your p-values come from the bootstrap variant known to over-reject in exactly your setting."
Unrestricted WCB, 21 effective clusters, half the identifying mass in three of them, and a footnote saying the null-imposed variant "could not be installed." **Why it matters:** the RQ2 result clears significance "by a wide margin" only under inference whose validity is asserted; with three months carrying half the mass, the honest degrees of freedom are small and the difference between bootstrap variants is first-order, not second. **How to address:** run null-imposed WCB (`boottest`/`fwildclusterboot`); add randomization inference over the d_t assignment; report a leave-three-top-months-out estimate; report the count of independent essential day-blocks behind the 12,513 intervals.

### RO5. "The nearest literatures are missing, and one of them already names your mechanism."
Inc-dec/redispatch gaming (Hirth–Schlecht), withholding-via-outages (Fogelberg–Lazarczyk; Bergler et al.), capacity-market strategy (Teirilä–Ritz), the divisible-good auction tradition (von der Fehr–Harbord; Fabra et al.), complex bids and commitment costs (Reguant), vertical/portfolio position (Bushnell–Mansur–Saravia; Ito–Reguant), and the NEM's own rebidding literature. **Why it matters:** the claimed contribution — "a margin that literature treats as exogenous" — is only sustainable once the neighbors are mapped; Reguant in particular supplies the innocent unit-commitment explanation for all-or-nothing availability that the paper must rule out (or absorb) explicitly, and Hirth–Schlecht supplies the strategic out-of-market-positioning frame the paper implicitly claims as novel. **How to address:** a rewritten positioning paragraph distinguishing this paper on (i) the administered, backward-looking payment price enabling a dose-response test, and (ii) the direction (presence) rather than redispatch (output) commodity; plus a §7 confrontation with commitment-cost economics: with start-up costs, what does the profitable-to-commit calculation look like, and does the 3.9% figure survive day-block rather than interval accounting?
